What Is a Geometry?

One object, two descriptions — Hilbert's and Klein's — and a ladder of levels that both descriptions climb. Nominate more structure and the group shrinks (Klein) as axiom groups accrue (Hilbert); at the metric rung, description becomes instrument. "Calibrated" is the double slit's name for that floor — a borrowed word worth examining, as is "metric" itself.

Not rivals — two descriptions of the same plane, certified both ways. Theorem 2: the synthetic axioms force the classical model, chain geometry over ℝ⊕ℝ. Theorem 5.4: every automorphism of that structure is induced by G. And crucially — the ladder below is not Klein's private property. Both descriptions climb the same rungs; each just names the ascent in its own coin.

Axis 2 — The ladder both methods climb

Tap a rung. Down the ladder: nominate more → the group shrinks (Klein) / an axiom group is added (Hilbert) → invariants sharpen. Below the crimson line, description becomes measurement. The classical tower has four rungs; the companion's §7 fuses the last two into Level 3, "Calibrated," because the rig pins the actual numbers.

Two borrowed words

"Calibrated" — a word from vision, not from Klein

The classical name for this rung is metric (or Euclidean). "Calibrated" comes from photogrammetry and computer vision, where a camera is calibrated once its intrinsics K are known — and knowing K is exactly what upgrades a reconstruction from projective/affine to metric. Vision built its own tower — projective → affine → metric → Euclidean — independently of Klein, named for reconstruction ambiguity, with calibrated ↔ metric its central equivalence (K ↔ the image of the absolute conic). This chart stands at the seam between Klein's tower and the vision tower: one ladder, two vocabularies. And the word earns its "instrument" tag in its roots — to calibrate is to fix an instrument's scale against a standard (calibre: the bore of a barrel, a manufacturing gauge). Of the three names for this rung, only "calibrated" names the act of pinning structure to numbers — which is why it belongs on the floor where description becomes measurement.

"Metric" — measure, and the absolute

"Metric" is overloaded, in three senses rarely distinguished: a metric space (a distance function — analysis), a Riemannian metric (an inner product on tangent vectors — differential geometry), and the Klein-level sense used here (the level at which length and angle are invariants). One root unites them — metron, measure: metric means measurable. And measurement is not primitive. In the Cayley–Klein view a metric is projective structure plus one nominated conic, the absolute, with distance and angle defined as cross-ratios against it (Cayley: "projective geometry is all geometry"). Theorem 7 is an instance: AR = (F₁,F₂;S,P) is a cross-ratio, and the three fiducials play the role of the absolute — the nominated figure that turns a measurement back into a cross-ratio. So metric = cross-ratio against a nominated absolute is the one sentence that makes metric, calibrated, and Theorem 7 the same idea.

The two axes are not independent. The ladder is a central spine both descriptions read — which reproduces this chart's own opening theme one floor down: "two methods, one object" becomes "two methods, one ladder," the same reconciliation applied to the stratification.
Caveats this chart now carries: "calibrated" is a vision word for the metric rung (apt, borrowed); metric and Euclidean are two rungs, separated by absolute scale, that §7 fuses into Level 3 because the rig pins the numbers; Hilbert's order (Group II) and continuity (Group V) don't slot cleanly onto the ladder, so the axiom-group map is a heuristic, sharpest for incidence / parallels / congruence; and "two methods" is a convenient pair, not an exhaustive one.
