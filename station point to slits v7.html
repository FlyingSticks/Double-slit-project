The complement, as one instrument. Perspective spends its fidelity on depth — an unbounded size cue, marginal splay at wide angles. Cross-slit spends it on breadth — gentler horizontal convergence (the splay halves), a metric aspect gauge, at the price of bowed depth lines and a bounded depth cue that saturates at Zv/Zh. So: deep-and-narrow scenes → keep the station point whole; broad-and-shallow, relief-like scenes with a solid background → pull it apart. The corridor preset shows the flip honestly: pull it apart there and the bow grows while the far end saturates — that scene belongs to perspective, exactly as your sheet says.

House plan is an imported SketchUp model — a floor plan cut at the 5-foot line, walls extruded to their tops. It is baked as a bird's-eye: you look straight down the wall-height axis, so the projection reveals the thickness of the walls as depth. Since the export's north wasn't fixed, the ⟳ orientation button rotates the plan in 90° steps — cycle to the reading you want. Then pull the station into slits to compare the one-point downward view with the cross-slit one on your own geometry.

The morph is exact: Zv = Zp − D/2, Zh = Zp + D/2; at D = 0 the map is identically the pinhole at Zp (checked to machine precision), at D = 100 it is the standing rig. Every picture here is a complete projection of the scene — faces painter-sorted, receding edges drawn as the true hyperbolic arcs.
