Violin portrait — rendered through the codec

0.00 0.0000

The violin as a surfaced body — arched spruce top, maple back and ribs, ebony fittings, purfling, f-holes cut through — in flat material color only, no texture, on a bare gray field. Photographic lighting is baked into each anchor's color at encode time; the payload is only (u, v, aspect, color). The body turns on three axes under your hand — drag for yaw and pitch, slide for roll, or let it spin — and every frame is its own photograph: encoded through the slits, thinned, and decoded — positions from aspect ratios, occlusion from painter-sorting by decoded depth. No z ever crosses the wire. The default view samples about a tenth of the film's packing bound; film-limit frame re-samples the surface anisotropically (fine along y, coarse along x — matched to the film's own aspect squeeze) and fills it.

Everything geometric — position, proportion, which surface hides which — comes out of the decode: Möbius inversion of each anchor's aspect ratio, then painter's ordering by the decoded z. The color is the photograph's job, carried per anchor. Nothing else is drawn: a flat gray field, no shadow, no vignette. Thinning is nearest-first, so merged anchors are the occluded skin — the film records what a camera would see. Raise σ and depth-sort speckle appears — the honest failure mode of a codec portrait. Verified in check violin portrait.js: the check script extracts and runs this page's own sampler and codec.
